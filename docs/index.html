The claim

OpenAI reports that GPT-5.6 Sol Ultra, running **64 parallel subagents for ~1 hour, generated a complete proof of the Cycle Double Cover Conjecture** — a ~50-year-old open problem in graph theory (posed by Seymour, 1979): every bridgeless graph admits a collection of cycles such that every edge lies in exactly two of them.

Specifics:

  • The proof PDF, published on OpenAI's CDN (10 July 2026), attributes authorship entirely to the model.
  • The argument reportedly reduces the problem via the 8-flow theorem and linear algebra over GF(3) (the three-element field).
  • OpenAI also released the prompt used.

Reported via OpenAI and covered on Hacker News, The Decoder, and others, July 2026.

Why it needs watching

  • Not the outsider profile — this is a flagship OpenAI lab announcement, AI used by its own makers as a marketing demonstration. The interesting question is narrow: does the proof actually hold?
  • No formal verification. Unlike the Fable-5 QAOA proof (mechanically checked end-to-end in Lean 4), this proof is **not peer-reviewed and not machine- verified** — it's a natural-language argument the community must check by hand, expected to take days to weeks.
  • Authorship dispute already brewing. Mathematicians are flagging possible gaps and questioning how much of the reasoning was the model's own vs. baked into the human prompter's search-guiding constraints (see the released prompt).
  • The conjecture is a graveyard. The Cycle Double Cover Conjecture has a history of announced "proofs" that later collapsed — strong prior for skepticism until specialists sign off.

Linear A deciphered as an extinct Semitic language

filed 2026-06-26 source →

Full review log

The claim

Tom Di Mino, a self-taught AI engineer and amateur linguist in the Hudson Valley, claims to have deciphered Linear A — the Bronze Age Minoan script undeciphered for over a century. He proposes it maps to an **extinct Semitic language** predating Biblical Hebrew, Arabic, and Aramaic.

Specifics:

  • Proposed readings for ~40 script signs, including 13 previously unknown.
  • A lexicon of 408 Linear A terms translated into English.
  • Identifies a verb root "nawaya" ("to dwell"); claims his solution also resolves open problems in the (already-deciphered) Linear B.
  • Built a suite of Python scripts using Claude Code to query and cross-reference the digitized Linear A corpus for systematic hypothesis testing. Has a draft 9-page manuscript.

Reported via AI Clambake and discussed on Hacker News, June 2026.

Why it needs watching

  • Classic outsider profile: amateur linguist + AI tooling, no academic affiliation. Linear A decipherment claims are a magnet for cranks — the test is whether epigraphers/Semiticists endorse it.
  • Author's claimed validation ("under review by linguistics experts at Rutgers and Cambridge") is unverified and not yet peer-reviewed.
  • Expert skepticism already raised: the corpus is tiny and fragmentary; we can't be sure all "Linear A" texts share one language; if it were Semitic, Greek substrate words should already show Semitic etymologies and largely don't (per commentary on HN).

Claude produced the core proof of a decade-old jamming critical-exponent identity (a + b = 1)

filed 2026-07-06 source →

Full review log

The claim

Giorgio Parisi (Nobel laureate) and Francesco Zamponi, both at La Sapienza University of Rome, report that Claude generated the essentially correct core idea for proving a conjecture the two of them first observed in 2014: that two critical exponents (a and b) in their mean-field model of jamming — the transition where a fluid-like system of particles abruptly becomes rigid — always sum to one, an identity that had gone unproven for over a decade.

Specifics:

  • Claude was first asked to reproduce the earlier numerical calculations, then to explain why the a + b = 1 relationship holds.
  • Per Zamponi: "Quite quickly, Claude came up with an initial idea that was essentially correct." The researchers then revised/tightened it into a full proof; the final solution turned out simpler than expected.
  • The proof confirms that two independent theoretical routes to jamming yield the same physical law.
  • Published 1 July 2026 in the *Journal of Statistical Mechanics: Theory and Experiment* (JSTAT), "A proof of an identity for the critical exponents of jamming."

Reported via EurekAlert, July 2026.

Why it needs watching

  • Not the usual outsider profile — the authors are among the most senior physicists in the field (Parisi won the 2021 Nobel partly for spin-glass / complex-systems work). This is AI-as-engine used *by* domain experts, so the "does the community accept it" test is weaker: the proof is already peer- reviewed and published in JSTAT. The open question is narrower — whether the AI-originated argument holds up under scrutiny and how much of the intellectual work was actually Claude's vs. the authors' revision.
  • Distinguish the *result* (already published, peer-reviewed identity) from the *narrative* (AI produced the key idea). The former is likely solid; the latter is the marketing-sensitive part worth re-checking as commentary accumulates.

Review log

  • ☑ +1 week (2026-07-13) — The underlying physics result is on solid ground: the paper is formally published and peer-reviewed in JSTAT (1 July 2026), and no domain physicist has challenged the a+b=1 identity itself. No formal critique or retraction signal. Status moved to partially-confirmed: the physics is confirmed, the AI-as-engine framing is still resting on self-report.
