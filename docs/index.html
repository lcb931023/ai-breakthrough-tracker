Claude produced the core proof of a decade-old jamming critical-exponent identity (a + b = 1)

added 2026-07-06 · next review 2026-07-13 (in 7d) · source: link

Review log

The claim

Giorgio Parisi (Nobel laureate) and Francesco Zamponi, both at La Sapienza

University of Rome, report that Claude generated the essentially correct

core idea for proving a conjecture the two of them first observed in 2014: that

two critical exponents (a and b) in their mean-field model of jamming

— the transition where a fluid-like system of particles abruptly becomes rigid —

always sum to one, an identity that had gone unproven for over a decade.

Specifics:

  • Claude was first asked to reproduce the earlier numerical calculations,

then to explain why the a + b = 1 relationship holds.

  • Per Zamponi: "Quite quickly, Claude came up with an initial idea that was

essentially correct." The researchers then revised/tightened it into a full

proof; the final solution turned out simpler than expected.

  • The proof confirms that two independent theoretical routes to jamming yield

the same physical law.

  • Published 1 July 2026 in the *Journal of Statistical Mechanics: Theory and

Experiment* (JSTAT), "A proof of an identity for the critical exponents of

jamming."

Reported via EurekAlert,

July 2026.

Why it needs watching

  • Not the usual outsider profile — the authors are among the most senior

physicists in the field (Parisi won the 2021 Nobel partly for spin-glass /

complex-systems work). This is AI-as-engine used *by* domain experts, so the

"does the community accept it" test is weaker: the proof is already peer-

reviewed and published in JSTAT. The open question is narrower — whether the

AI-originated argument holds up under scrutiny and how much of the intellectual

work was actually Claude's vs. the authors' revision.

  • Distinguish the *result* (already published, peer-reviewed identity) from the

*narrative* (AI produced the key idea). The former is likely solid; the latter

is the marketing-sensitive part worth re-checking as commentary accumulates.

unverified Linguistics / epigraphy (ancient script decipherment) outsider

Linear A deciphered as an extinct Semitic language

added 2026-06-26 · next review 2026-07-26 (in 20d) · source: link

Review log

The claim

Tom Di Mino, a self-taught AI engineer and amateur linguist in the Hudson

Valley, claims to have deciphered Linear A — the Bronze Age Minoan script

undeciphered for over a century. He proposes it maps to an **extinct Semitic

language** predating Biblical Hebrew, Arabic, and Aramaic.

Specifics:

  • Proposed readings for ~40 script signs, including 13 previously unknown.
  • A lexicon of 408 Linear A terms translated into English.
  • Identifies a verb root "nawaya" ("to dwell"); claims his solution also

resolves open problems in the (already-deciphered) Linear B.

  • Built a suite of Python scripts using Claude Code to query and

cross-reference the digitized Linear A corpus for systematic hypothesis

testing. Has a draft 9-page manuscript.

Reported via AI Clambake and

discussed on Hacker News,

June 2026.

Why it needs watching

  • Classic outsider profile: amateur linguist + AI tooling, no academic

affiliation. Linear A decipherment claims are a magnet for cranks — the test

is whether epigraphers/Semiticists endorse it.

  • Author's claimed validation ("under review by linguistics experts at Rutgers

and Cambridge") is unverified and not yet peer-reviewed.

  • Expert skepticism already raised: the corpus is tiny and fragmentary; we

can't be sure all "Linear A" texts share one language; if it were Semitic,

Greek substrate words should already show Semitic etymologies and largely

don't (per commentary on HN).
